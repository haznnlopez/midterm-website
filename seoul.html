Seoul, South Korea

I need to be in Seoul because I’m desperate to live in a world where 600-year-old dynasty secrets and neon-lit digital futures don't just coexist—they collide. I want to be the one stepping through the massive wooden gates of Gyeongbokgung Palace in a flowing silk hanbok, feeling the sudden, ancient silence fall over me while the glass skyscrapers of the modern city tower just beyond the walls. I need to lose myself in the narrow, winding stone alleys of Bukchon Hanok Village at dawn, then find myself three hours later in the industrial-chic cafes of Seongsu-dong, drinking an espresso in a renovated warehouse that feels like the birthplace of every global trend. I’m craving that late-night rush of Myeongdong, where the air is a symphony of sizzling street food and the glow of a thousand skincare shops, and I won't be satisfied until I’ve shared a communal table at Gwangjang Market, eating spicy tteokbokki under the watchful eye of a legendary "Netflix Lady." I have to stand at the top of N Seoul Tower as the city turns into a sea of electric pulsing lights, and I need to feel the raw, heartbreaking weight of history at the DMZ, just an hour away from the pop-infused energy of Gangnam. I don’t just want a trip; I need to be part of the heung—that infectious, soul-deep Korean joy—until I’ve experienced a city that manages to be the fastest-moving place on earth while never forgetting its heart.
